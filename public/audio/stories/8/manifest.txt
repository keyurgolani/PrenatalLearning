# Part 1 (~1981 chars)
# coreContent|1|coreContent-part1.mp3|The Postal System of Light! Imagine, sweet baby, a very special postal system. But instead of trucks and airplanes carrying letters, this postal system uses light itself; the fastest thing in the universe.! When you send a message on the internet, it doesn't travel as one big package. Instead, it's broken into tiny pieces called "packets"; like tearing a letter into small strips. Each packet is labeled with where it came from and where it's going, just like an address on an envelope.! These packets then race through wires, bounce off satellites, and zip through the air, each finding its own path to the destination. Some might go through New York, others through London, others through Tokyo. But they all arrive at the same place, where they're put back together in the right order.! This, little one, is how the internet works; a postal system made of light, where billions of tiny messages crisscross the globe every second.! The Birth of a Dream: ARPANET! Let's travel back in time, little one, to when the internet was just a dream.! In the 1960s, during a time called the Cold War, scientists in America had a problem. They had big, expensive computers at different universities, but these computers couldn't talk to each other. Each computer was like an island, alone and isolated.! A group of brilliant people at a place called DARPA (the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency) had an idea: what if we could connect these computers together? What if they could share information, like friends passing notes?! On October 29, 1969, something magical happened. A student at UCLA tried to send a message to a computer at Stanford, about 350 miles away. The message was supposed to be "LOGIN." But after typing just "L" and "O," the system crashed! The first message ever sent on what would become the internet was simply "LO."! It wasn't a perfect start, but it was a start. They fixed the problem, and soon computers at four universities were talking to each other.
# Part 2 (~1985 chars)
# coreContent|2|coreContent-part2.mp3|This little network was called ARPANET, and it was the seed that would grow into the internet we know today.! Packet Switching: The Clever Trick! Here's the clever trick that makes the internet work, little one. It's called packet switching.! Imagine you want to send a beautiful painting to a friend across the country. In the old days, you'd have to find one truck to carry the whole painting from your house to your friend's house. If that truck got stuck in traffic or broke down, your painting would be delayed.! But what if you could cut the painting into a thousand tiny pieces, put each piece in a separate envelope, and send them all at once through different routes? Some might go by truck, some by train, some by airplane. Even if some routes are slow or blocked, most pieces would get through quickly. And your friend could put all the pieces back together at the end.! This is packet switching! Your message is broken into packets, each packet finds its own way through the network, and they're reassembled at the destination. It's more reliable, more efficient, and much, much faster than sending everything through one path.! The Language of the Internet: TCP/IP! For computers to talk to each other, they need to speak the same language. Just like you and I speak English, computers on the internet speak a language called TCP/IP.! TCP stands for Transmission Control Protocol. Think of TCP as a very careful librarian. When you send a message, TCP breaks it into packets and numbers each one. When the packets arrive at the destination, TCP checks that all the packets arrived and puts them back in the right order. If any packets got lost along the way, TCP asks for them to be sent again. TCP makes sure your message arrives complete and correct.! IP stands for Internet Protocol. Think of IP as the addressing system; like street addresses for computers. Every device on the internet has a unique IP address, a series of numbers that identifies exactly where it is.
# Part 3 (~1921 chars)
# coreContent|3|coreContent-part3.mp3|When you send a message, IP makes sure each packet knows where it's going and how to get there.! Together, TCP/IP is like a postal service with a perfect memory; it never loses a letter, never delivers to the wrong address, and always puts your message back together perfectly.! Routers: The Traffic Directors! Now, little one, let's meet the unsung heroes of the internet: routers.! Imagine a busy city with millions of cars, all trying to get to different places. At every intersection, there's a traffic director who knows all the roads and can tell each car the best way to go. "You want to go to the library? Turn left here, then right at the next light."! Routers are the traffic directors of the internet. When a packet arrives at a router, the router looks at the destination address and decides the best path to send it. Should it go through this cable or that one? Through this network or that one? The router makes these decisions in a fraction of a second, millions of times per second.! And here's the amazing part: routers talk to each other constantly, sharing information about which paths are fast and which are slow, which are working and which are broken. If one path becomes congested, routers automatically find another way. The internet is always adapting, always finding the best route.! The Physical Internet: Cables Under the Sea! The internet might seem invisible, but it has a physical body, little one. Let me show you.! Stretching across the bottom of every ocean are thick cables, some as wide as a garden hose, carrying light signals from continent to continent. These undersea cables carry 99% of all international internet traffic. Right now, as we speak, your messages might be traveling through cables lying on the ocean floor, thousands of feet below the waves.! On land, the internet travels through fiber optic cables; thin strands of glass that carry information as pulses of light.
# Part 4 (~1999 chars)
# coreContent|4|coreContent-part4.mp3|These cables run under streets, along railroad tracks, and through underground tunnels. They connect cities to cities, countries to countries.! And in the air, the internet travels as radio waves; through WiFi in your home, through cell towers on hilltops, through satellites orbiting high above the Earth. Some satellites are so far away that a signal takes a quarter of a second to reach them and come back.! All of this; the cables, the towers, the satellites; forms the physical body of the internet. It's a body that spans the entire planet.! DNS: The Internet's Phone Book! Here's a problem, little one: computers use numbers to find each other (like 172.217.14.206), but humans prefer names (like google. com). How do we bridge this gap?! The answer is DNS; the Domain Name System. Think of DNS as a giant phone book for the internet.! When you type "google. com" into your browser, your computer doesn't actually know where Google is. So it asks a DNS server: "What's the number for google. com?" The DNS server looks it up and replies: "That's 172.217.14.206." Now your computer knows where to send its message.! DNS servers are spread all around the world, and they constantly share information with each other. When a new website is created, its name and number are added to the DNS system, and soon every DNS server in the world knows about it.! It's like having a phone book that updates itself automatically, that everyone in the world shares, and that can look up any name in a fraction of a second.! The World Wide Web: Tim's Gift to Humanity! Now, little one, let's meet one of the most generous people in history: Tim Berners-Lee.! In 1989, Tim was working at a science laboratory in Switzerland called CERN. Scientists there had a problem: they had lots of information on different computers, but it was hard to find and share. Tim had an idea: what if documents could contain links to other documents? What if you could click on a word and instantly jump to related information?!
# Part 5 (~1969 chars)
# coreContent|5|coreContent-part5.mp3|Tim created three things that changed the world: HTML (a way to write documents with links), HTTP (a way for computers to share these documents), and the first web browser (a program to view them). Together, these inventions became the World Wide Web.! But here's what makes Tim truly special: he gave it all away for free. He could have patented his inventions and become incredibly wealthy. Instead, he said the web should belong to everyone. He wanted it to be a gift to humanity.! And what a gift it was! The web grew from one website in 1991 to millions by the end of the decade. Today, there are nearly two billion websites, and the web has transformed how we learn, work, play, and connect.! How a Web Page Reaches You! Let's follow a web page on its journey to your screen, little one.! You type a website address into your browser. Your computer asks DNS for the IP address. Then it sends a request across the internet: "Please send me this web page."! The request travels through your home router, through your internet provider's network, through undersea cables or satellite links, through more routers and networks, until it reaches the server where the website lives.! The server receives your request and prepares the web page. It gathers the text, the images, the videos, the code that makes things move and respond. Then it breaks all of this into packets and sends them back to you.! The packets race across the world, each finding its own path. They arrive at your computer, where they're reassembled into the complete web page. Your browser reads the code and draws the page on your screen.! All of this happens in less than a second. A request travels around the world and back, and a page appears before your eyes. It's like magic, but it's really the work of millions of computers, cables, and clever ideas all working together.! Email: Letters at the Speed of Light! Before the internet, little one, sending a letter across the world took weeks.

It's a way to stay connected with people you love, no matter how far apart you are.! Video Calls: Seeing Across the World! Here's something that would have seemed like pure magic to your great-great-grandparents, little one: video calls.! When you make a video call, your camera captures your image many times per second. Each image is compressed (made smaller) and broken into packets. These packets race across the internet to the other person's device, where they're reassembled and displayed on the screen.! At the same time, the other person's image is traveling back to you. And both of your voices are being captured, compressed, sent, and played back. All of this happens so fast that it feels like you're in the same room, even if you're on opposite sides of the planet.! Video calls let grandparents see their grandchildren grow up, even from far away. They let friends stay close even when life takes them to different places. They let people work together even when they can't be together.! The Internet of Things: Everything Connected! The internet started by connecting computers, little one.
